A batch of beer is a process running unattended for two weeks in a sealed vessel you can't log into. You set the initial conditions, you arm your controls, and then you let billions of organisms execute a workload you cannot pause. If that doesn't sound like infrastructure to you, you've never run either.

People think brewing is a craft of intuition. Grandfather's nose, a wooden spoon, a feeling. That's the costume. Underneath it, brewing is the same brain as everything else I do: measurement, repeatability, control loops, tight tolerances, and a written process you can trust at 2am when you're tired and the thing is misbehaving.

The competition ferments on vibes. I ferment on data. That's not arrogance — it's just the part where I admit the yeast doesn't care about my feelings.

## Everything is a setpoint

Beer is a stack of variables that all want to drift. Temperature wants to climb. Sugars want to convert at rates that track heat you didn't hold steady. Microbes want to throw a party slightly different from the one you invited them to. Left alone, the system finds an equilibrium — just not the one you wanted.

So you instrument it. You measure going in and you measure coming out, and the delta between those two numbers tells you what actually happened versus what you hoped happened. Hope is not a measurement. A reading is a measurement.

> You can't control what you refuse to measure. You can only be surprised by it later, in a glass, in front of people.

The discipline isn't exotic. Hold your fermentation temperature in a band instead of letting the room decide. Sanitize like it's a control with consequences, because it is — contamination is just an intruder who got in through a step you skipped, and like any intrusion, it costs you most when you didn't see it happen. Pitch a known quantity of yeast instead of a vague handful. None of this is romance. All of it is why the next batch tastes like the last good one.

## The control loop is the whole game

Open loop: you do the steps, you cross your fingers, you find out at the end. Closed loop: you measure, you compare against the setpoint, you correct, you measure again. The first one is a hobby. The second is engineering, and it's the only way you ever make the same great beer twice on purpose.

This is the part that separates a fluke from a process. Anybody can stumble into one good batch. Reproducing it is the actual skill, and reproduction is impossible without a feedback loop and honest notes.

Which brings me to the notes.

## The log is the product

Write it down. All of it. The numbers, the times, the temperatures, the thing you changed on a whim, the thing that went wrong and how you caught it. Not because you're precious about it — because future-you is an idiot with no memory and a confident opinion, and the only thing that can overrule him is a written record.

A batch with no notes is a batch you got away with. It taught you nothing. You can't tune what you didn't capture, can't roll back to a known-good config you never wrote down, can't tell whether your latest "improvement" actually improved anything or just got lucky. The brew log is the operating manual. The beer is the build artifact. People taste the artifact and assume that's the work. The log is the work.

I keep mine tight enough that I could hand it to a stranger and they'd reproduce the result. That's the bar. If your process only runs inside your own head, you don't have a process. You have a personality.

## Tolerances are a love language

Here's the heel turn, and it's the warmest thing I'll say all day. Caring about tight tolerances is not joyless. It's the opposite. The romance is in doing it properly — in the quiet confidence of pouring something months later and knowing exactly why it tastes the way it does. Not magic. Not a good day. A system you understood and ran clean.

Anyone can make beer once. Most people, frankly, will. The discipline is making the beer you meant to make, again, after you've forgotten the first one — and proving on paper how you got there.

This is Vol. 002, and it runs on the same rule the merch did: one run, no restock. But the recipe, the log, the loop — those persist. The yeast does the fermenting. I do the engineering. We split the credit unevenly, and neither of us complains.
